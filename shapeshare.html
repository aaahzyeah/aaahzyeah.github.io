ShapeShare: A Conceptual Toolkit Based on a Modular Shape-Changing Interface for Supporting Remote Co-Design

HCI Research

Remote collaboration is a challenge for physical product designers, and especially limits the mutual communication of the ‘thinking through prototyping’ approach. Inspired by the NURBSforms and self-shape sensing technologies, we proposed ShapeShare, a conceptual toolkit for supporting designers to build a design and share it physically with remote collaborators. By using stop-motion videos and low-fi prototypes as probes, the concept were evaluated through questionnaires and workshops with a simplified version of the toolkit. The result shows the potential to bridge the gap between digital and physical, especially in the exploration phase of remote co-design.

Date: 11/2020 --> 01/2021 (3 months)

Tag: Course: Designing user interfaces with emerging technology (DBM170)

Expertise:Technology and Realization, User and Society.

Background & Problem

Designers tend to use a ‘thinking through prototyping’ method when collaborating, as it facilitates communication, discussion and mutual understanding of form, scale and physical proportions. The increase in globalization and the restrictions caused by the COVID-19 pandemic, have led to an increase in remote and distributed collaboration, thus limiting the possibility to use a ‘thinking through prototyping’ approach and exchange between collaborators.

Related Work

Related to this problems, research effort have focused on shape transmission by remotely rendering the shape of people and objects. However these methods are limited to linear actuation and are therefore limited in their shape expression. Additionally, works like ‘physical telepresence’ request substantial resources and costs for prototyping.

A more cost efficient approach to surface actuation are the use of shape memory alloys. These alloys are used in research prototypes to actuate modular programmable surfaces like the work NURBSforms. This technology shows potential for the application in remotes collaboration, however current use cases are limited to 3D nurbs modeling.

Tech Exploration

In order to address these problems, we have used NURBSforms as base for our design. Using this technology, we were able to create simple functional prototypes that allowed us to explore different scenarios. The exploration can be seen in the video.

The Final Concept

We propose ShapeShare, a conceptual toolkit that enables designers to share physical explorations of form, scale, and spatial proportions in remote design collaborations. The toolkit consists of several shape memory strips of different lengths and covers of different shapes and materials.

Two versions of the toolkit (a) in the conceptual toolkit, with shape memory alloy and electronic components embedded, (b) the simple and non-electronic toolkit for user evaluation.

Interaction Flow

Creating various shapes with different combinations of elements in the toolkit, and input with gesture. (a) a triangle shape, (b) a square shape, (c) a disc shape, (d) a cylinder shape.

Evaluation

The potential and usability of ShapeShare was evaluated using 1) an online questionnaire and 2) an online workshop evaluation, both aimed at gaining quantitative and qualitative results.

questionnaires.The results of part three showed the concept makes communicating design ideas more tangible, which reduces vagueness, especially when the other designer can duplicate the same shape easily. It also helps to quickly adjust the prototype or to explore more options, and reduces the time spent on a 3d model or verbally explaining. However, some of the participants were concerned the application is still limited to general shapes.
